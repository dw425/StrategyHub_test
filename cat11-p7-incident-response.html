IR Fundamentals

Core concepts and foundational knowledge

What is Incident Response?

Incident response (IR) is the organized, systematic approach to addressing and managing the aftermath of a security breach, cyberattack, or other security event. The goal is to handle the situation in a way that limits damage, reduces recovery time and costs, preserves evidence for potential legal action, and prevents future incidents through lessons learned.

A well-prepared incident response capability is essential for every organization. Security incidents are inevitable‚Äîthe question is not if you'll experience one, but when. Organizations with mature IR programs consistently demonstrate faster containment, lower breach costs, and better regulatory outcomes.

The Business Case for Incident Response

According to IBM's Cost of a Data Breach Report 2023:

  • $4.45 million: Average total cost of a data breach globally
  • $1.49 million: Average savings for organizations with tested IR plans vs. those without
  • $1.02 million: Additional cost for breaches taking longer than 200 days to identify and contain
  • 277 days: Average time to identify and contain a breach (organizations with IR teams: 214 days)
  • 51%: Organizations that experienced a breach in the past two years

Key Terminology

Clear terminology prevents confusion during high-stress incidents:

  • Event: Any observable occurrence in a system or network. The vast majority of events are routine and benign‚Äîa user logging in, a file being accessed, a process starting.
  • Alert: An event or set of events flagged by security tools as potentially suspicious or anomalous. Alerts require triage to determine if they represent a real threat.
  • Incident: A confirmed security event that violates security policies, threatens confidentiality/integrity/availability of data, or requires a formal response. Incidents trigger the IR process.
  • Breach: A specific type of incident where sensitive, protected, or confidential data has been accessed, stolen, or exposed by an unauthorized party. Breaches typically trigger notification requirements.
  • Compromise: When a system, account, or asset has been successfully attacked and is under some degree of attacker control.
üí° Key Distinction
All breaches are incidents, but not all incidents are breaches. A ransomware attack that encrypts systems but doesn't exfiltrate data is an incident but may not be a breach requiring notification. However, many regulations now consider ransomware a reportable breach regardless of exfiltration.

The IR Mindset

Effective incident response requires a specific mindset that should be cultivated across the organization:

  • Assume Breach: Operate under the assumption that attackers may already be inside your environment. This drives better detection, faster response, and more resilient architecture.
  • Preserve Evidence: In the rush to stop an attack, it's tempting to immediately wipe systems or restore from backup. Resist this urge‚Äîforensic evidence may be crucial for understanding the attack, meeting regulatory requirements, and potential legal action.
  • Communicate Clearly: Confusion and miscommunication cause delays and mistakes. Establish clear communication channels, use consistent terminology, and provide regular updates even when there's no new information.
  • Learn Continuously: Every incident, whether real or exercise, is a learning opportunity. Conduct blameless post-mortems focused on process improvement, not individual fault.
  • Stay Calm: Panic leads to poor decisions. Follow established playbooks, take time to think before acting, and don't let urgency override sound judgment.

Incident Response Frameworks

Several established frameworks guide incident response programs:

  • NIST SP 800-61: Computer Security Incident Handling Guide from the National Institute of Standards and Technology. The most widely adopted framework, defining four phases: Preparation, Detection & Analysis, Containment/Eradication/Recovery, and Post-Incident Activity.
  • SANS Incident Handler's Handbook: Practical, hands-on guidance organized into six phases: Preparation, Identification, Containment, Eradication, Recovery, and Lessons Learned.
  • ISO/IEC 27035: International standard for information security incident management, providing a structured approach aligned with ISO 27001.
  • CISA Incident Response Playbooks: Operational playbooks from the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency covering specific incident types.

IR Program Maturity Levels

Assess your current capability and plan improvements:

  • Level 1 - Ad Hoc: No formal IR process. Response is reactive and improvised. No dedicated team or tools.
  • Level 2 - Defined: Basic IR plan exists. Roles are assigned but not practiced. Limited tools and documentation.
  • Level 3 - Managed: Documented playbooks for common incidents. Regular training and exercises. Dedicated IR team. SIEM and basic forensic tools.
  • Level 4 - Measured: Metrics tracked and reported. Integration with threat intelligence. Automation for routine tasks. Regular red team exercises.
  • Level 5 - Optimized: Continuous improvement driven by metrics. Advanced automation and orchestration. Proactive threat hunting. Industry-leading response times.

Phase 1: Preparation (Detailed)

Preparation is the most important phase‚Äîit determines how effectively you'll handle actual incidents. This phase is ongoing and should consume 80% of your IR program effort.

IR Plan Development
  • Define scope, objectives, and authority of the IR program
  • Document incident classification criteria and severity levels
  • Establish escalation procedures and decision authorities
  • Define roles, responsibilities, and contact information
  • Document communication protocols (internal, external, regulatory)
  • Identify critical assets and systems requiring priority protection
  • Review and update the plan at least annually
Team Building and Training
  • Identify and formally assign IR team members with clear roles
  • Ensure 24/7 coverage through on-call rotations
  • Provide technical training on forensics, malware analysis, and investigation
  • Train on legal requirements, evidence handling, and communication
  • Conduct regular tabletop exercises (quarterly recommended)
  • Participate in external exercises and simulations
  • Cross-train team members to avoid single points of failure
Tool and Infrastructure Readiness
  • Deploy and tune SIEM for effective alerting
  • Implement EDR/XDR across all endpoints
  • Establish forensic workstations with required tools
  • Maintain offline backups tested for restoration
  • Prepare "jump kits" with portable forensic tools
  • Establish secure out-of-band communication channels
  • Pre-stage containment scripts and automation
  • Document network diagrams and asset inventories
External Relationships
  • Establish retainer with external IR firm for surge capacity
  • Identify local FBI/Secret Service field office contacts
  • Know your cyber insurance policy and carrier contacts
  • Build relationships with industry ISACs for threat intelligence
  • Establish communication with legal counsel (internal and external)
  • Identify and pre-approve crisis communications resources

Phase 2: Detection & Analysis (Detailed)

The detection phase determines how quickly you identify incidents. Faster detection means less damage, lower costs, and better outcomes.

Detection Sources
  • Automated: SIEM alerts, EDR detections, IDS/IPS, WAF, CASB, email security
  • Proactive: Threat hunting, vulnerability scanning, penetration testing
  • External: Threat intelligence feeds, law enforcement, vendor notifications
  • Internal: Employee reports, help desk tickets, suspicious activity reports
  • Third-party: Customer complaints, partner notifications, media reports
Alert Triage Process
  1. Initial review: Examine alert details, affected systems, and contextual information
  2. Validation: Confirm the alert represents real activity (not false positive)
  3. Enrichment: Gather additional context from logs, threat intel, and asset inventory
  4. Correlation: Look for related alerts or indicators across systems
  5. Classification: Determine if this is an incident requiring formal response
  6. Severity assignment: Apply severity criteria to prioritize response
  7. Documentation: Create incident ticket with all findings
Indicators of Compromise (IOCs) to Look For
  • Unusual outbound network connections (especially to known-bad IPs)
  • Unexpected processes or services running
  • Failed authentication attempts followed by success
  • Access at unusual times or from unusual locations
  • Large data transfers or unusual data access patterns
  • Registry or configuration changes
  • Disabled security tools or modified logs
  • Suspicious scheduled tasks or startup items

Phase 3: Containment (Detailed)

Containment stops the bleeding. The goal is to prevent further damage while preserving evidence and maintaining business operations where possible.

Short-Term Containment (Immediate Actions)
  • Isolate affected systems from the network (but don't power off)
  • Block known malicious IPs, domains, and hashes
  • Disable compromised accounts
  • Revoke active sessions and tokens
  • Implement emergency firewall rules
  • Quarantine affected endpoints via EDR
  • Preserve memory and disk state for forensics
Long-Term Containment (Sustained Actions)
  • Implement network segmentation to limit lateral movement
  • Deploy additional monitoring on adjacent systems
  • Reset passwords for potentially affected accounts
  • Patch critical vulnerabilities being exploited
  • Implement temporary compensating controls
  • Set up clean systems for continued operations if needed
‚ö†Ô∏è Evidence Preservation
Do NOT immediately power off or reimage systems. Memory contains crucial evidence (running processes, network connections, malware in RAM) that is lost on shutdown. Isolate at the network level instead.

Phase 4: Eradication (Detailed)

Eradication removes all traces of the attacker from the environment. Incomplete eradication often leads to re-compromise.

Eradication Steps
  • Identify and remove all malware, backdoors, and attacker tools
  • Identify and close the initial access vector
  • Reset all potentially compromised credentials (not just confirmed ones)
  • Revoke and re-issue certificates if PKI was compromised
  • Patch all vulnerabilities being exploited
  • Remove unauthorized accounts or privilege escalations
  • Clean or rebuild affected systems
  • Scan environment for persistence mechanisms
Verification
  • Scan all systems with updated signatures
  • Review logs for signs of continued attacker activity
  • Verify no unauthorized network connections
  • Confirm all IOCs have been addressed
  • Have a second analyst verify eradication completeness

Phase 5: Recovery (Detailed)

Recovery restores systems to normal operation while implementing lessons learned and enhanced monitoring.

Recovery Planning
  • Determine recovery sequence based on business priority
  • Identify whether systems should be restored or rebuilt
  • Validate backup integrity before restoration
  • Plan for phased return to production
  • Define success criteria for each recovery phase
Recovery Execution
  • Restore from known-good backups (verify they predate compromise)
  • Rebuild systems from golden images if backups are suspect
  • Apply all security patches before returning to production
  • Implement additional security controls identified during investigation
  • Test system functionality before enabling user access
  • Gradually restore network connectivity while monitoring
  • Implement enhanced logging and monitoring
Enhanced Monitoring
  • Increase logging verbosity on recovered systems
  • Deploy additional network monitoring
  • Create alerts for known attacker TTPs from this incident
  • Monitor for re-compromise indicators
  • Maintain enhanced monitoring for 30-90 days post-recovery

Phase 6: Lessons Learned (Detailed)

The lessons learned phase closes the loop and ensures continuous improvement. This phase is often skipped due to fatigue or pressure to "move on"‚Äîdon't skip it.

Post-Incident Review Meeting

Conduct within 1-2 weeks while memories are fresh. Include all participants in the response.

  • Review complete incident timeline
  • What went well? What should we keep doing?
  • What didn't go well? What should we change?
  • Were playbooks and procedures followed? Were they effective?
  • Were there communication issues or gaps?
  • What tools or resources were missing?
  • What training gaps were identified?
‚úÖ Blameless Post-Mortems
Focus on process and system improvements, not individual blame. People make mistakes when processes allow mistakes to happen. Ask "how did our systems allow this?" not "who caused this?"
Documentation and Reporting
  • Complete incident report with timeline, impact, and actions taken
  • Executive summary for leadership
  • Technical report with IOCs and TTPs for threat intelligence
  • Regulatory reports if required (breach notifications)
  • Insurance documentation for claims
Improvement Actions
  • Update detection rules based on attack patterns
  • Revise playbooks based on lessons learned
  • Address identified control gaps
  • Improve training based on skill gaps
  • Update contact lists and escalation procedures
  • Track remediation items to completion

Severity Assessment Criteria

Use these factors to determine incident severity. When factors point to different severities, use the highest indicated level.

Data Impact Assessment

  • Critical (SEV-1): Mass PII exposure (10,000+ records), financial data, healthcare records (PHI), credentials, or trade secrets confirmed compromised
  • High (SEV-2): Limited PII exposure (100-10,000 records), sensitive internal data, customer data at risk
  • Medium (SEV-3): Internal data exposure, non-sensitive customer data, limited scope
  • Low (SEV-4): No sensitive data involved, public information only

System Impact Assessment

  • Critical (SEV-1): Production systems down, customer-facing services affected, core infrastructure compromised, domain controller or identity provider compromised
  • High (SEV-2): Important business systems affected, potential for production impact, critical workstations compromised
  • Medium (SEV-3): Non-critical systems affected, limited business impact, contained to specific systems
  • Low (SEV-4): Individual workstation, test/dev systems, no production impact

Scope Assessment

  • Critical (SEV-1): Organization-wide impact, multiple business units, customers affected, media attention likely
  • High (SEV-2): Department-wide impact, multiple teams affected, potential to spread
  • Medium (SEV-3): Team or small group affected, contained spread
  • Low (SEV-4): Individual user or system, no spread potential

Threat Actor Assessment

  • Critical (SEV-1): Nation-state or APT indicators, active attacker presence, sophisticated tradecraft
  • High (SEV-2): Targeted attack indicators, criminal organization, active exploitation
  • Medium (SEV-3): Opportunistic attack, commodity malware, unclear attribution
  • Low (SEV-4): Automated/untargeted, script kiddie, blocked attempt

Regulatory Impact Assessment

  • Critical (SEV-1): Mandatory breach notification required, regulatory investigation likely, significant fines possible
  • High (SEV-2): May require notification, regulatory attention possible
  • Medium (SEV-3): Internal reporting required, unlikely external notification
  • Low (SEV-4): No regulatory implications
üö® Automatic SEV-1 Triggers
The following situations automatically warrant SEV-1 classification regardless of other factors: Active ransomware encryption, confirmed data exfiltration, compromised domain controller, production database breach, regulatory notification threshold exceeded, media coverage of incident, law enforcement contact about your organization.

Severity Escalation and De-escalation

Severity levels can change as more information becomes available:

  • Escalate when: Impact is greater than initially assessed, additional systems found compromised, data exposure confirmed, attacker activity continues, business impact increases
  • De-escalate when: Investigation confirms limited scope, containment is successful, impact is less than feared, threat is neutralized
  • Authority: Incident Commander can escalate at any time. De-escalation requires IC approval and should be documented with rationale.

Purpose: This playbook provides step-by-step guidance for responding to ransomware attacks, including variants that combine encryption with data exfiltration (double extortion). 1
üö® IMMEDIATE: Isolate Affected Systems
0-15 minutes
The single most important action is stopping the spread. Ransomware propagates rapidly through networks‚Äîevery minute of delay means more encrypted systems. DO NOT power off systems as this destroys forensic evidence.
Execution Steps
  1. Physically disconnect network cables from confirmed infected systems (do not shut down)
  2. Disable Wi-Fi adapters on affected systems
  3. Block affected systems at switch port level if possible
  4. Quarantine affected endpoints via EDR (CrowdStrike: network contain)
  5. Block SMB (ports 445, 139) between network segments
  6. Disable any shared drives or network shares that might spread infection
  7. Consider temporarily isolating entire affected network segments
  8. Document the exact time of each isolation action

2

15-30 minutes
Declare a SEV-1 incident and mobilize the full response team. Ransomware incidents require all-hands response with executive involvement due to business impact and potential ransom decisions.
Execution Steps
  1. Page on-call Incident Commander and IR team via PagerDuty
  2. Notify CISO immediately (phone call, not just email)
  3. Alert CEO, CFO, and General Counsel of SEV-1 incident
  4. Establish war room (physical or virtual bridge line)
  5. Create dedicated Slack channel: #incident-ransomware-[date]
  6. Begin incident documentation in ticketing system
  7. Assign Scribe to maintain real-time timeline

30 min - 2 hours

Execution Steps
  1. Create inventory of all confirmed and suspected infected systems
  2. Check SIEM for indicators of lateral movement (SMB, WMI, PsExec)

1-4 hours (parallel)

  6. Document network connections from infected systems

2-8 hours
Protect systems that haven't been infected and prevent reinfection after recovery. Attackers often have multiple access vectors and may attempt re-entry.
Execution Steps
  1. Reset passwords for ALL accounts, prioritizing privileged accounts
  2. Revoke and regenerate all service account credentials
  3. Force re-authentication of all active sessions
  4. If AD is compromised, reset KRBTGT password (twice, 10+ hours apart)
  5. Disable any accounts showing suspicious activity
  6. Block known malicious IPs, domains, and file hashes
  7. Deploy emergency patches for exploitation vectors if identified
  8. Disable remote access tools until investigation complete
  9. Increase logging and monitoring on all systems
  10. Verify backup systems are isolated and intact

6
üí∞ Evaluate Recovery Options
4-24 hours
Assess all options for recovery including backups, decryption tools, and the ransom option (as last resort). This decision requires executive and legal input.
Execution Steps
  1. Verify backup integrity‚Äîtest restoration of sample systems
  2. Determine backup age and acceptable data loss (RPO assessment)
  3. Check if free decryption tools are available for identified variant
  4. Calculate cost of recovery from backup vs. downtime impact
  5. If considering ransom: engage professional negotiators through legal counsel
  6. Verify if paying ransom violates OFAC sanctions
  7. Document decision rationale regardless of path chosen
  8. Obtain executive sign-off on recovery approach

7
üîß Eradicate and Recover
24-72+ hours
Remove all traces of the ransomware and restore systems to operation. This is typically done by rebuilding systems from known-good sources rather than attempting to clean infected systems.
Execution Steps
  1. Prioritize recovery order based on business criticality
  2. Rebuild affected systems from golden images (preferred over cleaning)
  3. Restore data from backups verified to predate infection
  4. Apply all security patches before returning systems to network
  5. Re-join systems to domain with new computer accounts
  6. Install and verify EDR is active on all recovered systems
  7. Implement additional security controls identified during investigation
  8. Restore network connectivity in phases with monitoring
  9. Validate system functionality before enabling user access
  10. Document all recovery actions and system changes

Within 2 weeks
Complete documentation, conduct lessons learned, and implement improvements to prevent recurrence.
Execution Steps
  1. Complete incident timeline and final report
  2. Conduct blameless post-mortem with all responders
  3. File regulatory notifications if required (assess breach thresholds)
  4. Submit cyber insurance claim with documentation
  5. Report to FBI IC3 (ic3.gov) and consider engaging FBI field office
  6. Update detection rules based on observed TTPs
  7. Implement identified security improvements
  8. Review and update this playbook based on lessons learned
  9. Conduct additional training if gaps were identified
  10. Maintain enhanced monitoring for 90 days post-recovery

Data Breach Response Playbook

Protocol for confirmed data exfiltration

üîì Data Breach Response
üìã Version 2.8 ‚ö° Severity: SEV-1/SEV-2 ‚è±Ô∏è Notification Deadline: 72 hours (GDPR)

Purpose: This playbook guides response when sensitive data has been or may have been accessed, exfiltrated, or exposed by unauthorized parties. Key concerns include understanding what data was affected, meeting notification requirements, and preventing further exposure.

1
üîç Confirm and Scope the Breach
0-4 hours
Determine if a breach actually occurred, what data was affected, and the scope of exposure. This assessment drives notification requirements and response actions.
Execution Steps
  1. Identify the source of the breach indication (alert, report, external notification)
  2. Determine what systems or data stores were accessed
  3. Identify the data types involved (PII, PHI, financial, credentials, etc.)
  4. Estimate the number of records/individuals affected
  5. Determine the time window of unauthorized access
  6. Identify whether data was viewed, copied, or exfiltrated
  7. Check for evidence of data appearing externally (dark web, paste sites)
  8. Document all findings with supporting evidence
SIEM Log Analysis DLP Alerts Dark Web Monitoring Database Audit Logs
2
‚öñÔ∏è Engage Legal and Assess Notification Requirements
Immediately
Legal counsel must be involved immediately to preserve privilege, assess notification obligations, and guide response. Notification clocks start ticking once a breach is discovered.
Execution Steps
  1. Notify General Counsel and engage outside breach counsel if needed
  2. Conduct initial communications under attorney-client privilege
  3. Determine applicable regulations based on data types and affected individuals
  4. Identify notification deadlines: GDPR (72 hrs), HIPAA (60 days), state laws (varies)
  5. Assess whether breach meets notification thresholds
  6. Identify all jurisdictions where affected individuals reside
  7. Determine regulatory bodies requiring notification
  8. Begin drafting notification letters and regulatory reports
‚ö†Ô∏è The 72-hour GDPR notification clock starts when you become "aware" of a breach. Document the exact time of awareness.
3
üõë Contain and Stop Further Exposure
0-8 hours
Take immediate actions to prevent additional data from being accessed or exfiltrated.
Execution Steps
  1. Disable compromised accounts and revoke access tokens
  2. Block attacker IPs and domains at firewall
  3. Isolate affected systems from network if needed
  4. Remove any publicly exposed data (if applicable)
  5. Request takedown of data posted externally
  6. Reset credentials for accounts with access to affected data
  7. Disable affected API keys or integrations
  8. Implement additional access controls on affected data stores
4
üìä Conduct Detailed Impact Assessment
4-48 hours
Perform thorough analysis to understand exactly what was compromised. This determines notification content and remediation needs.
Execution Steps
  1. Query affected databases/systems to identify specific records accessed
  2. Generate list of affected individuals with contact information
  3. Categorize data types for each affected individual
  4. Assess sensitivity level and harm potential for exposed data
  5. Determine if data was encrypted at rest (may reduce harm)
  6. Identify any data that was tokenized or masked
  7. Document the attack vector and attacker actions
  8. Prepare data for notification vendor (if using third party)
5
üì¢ Execute Notifications
Per regulatory deadlines

Digital Forensics

üíæ
Volatile Evidence Collection
Capture evidence that will be lost when system is powered off. Collect in order of volatility‚Äîmost volatile first.

Network-based evidence for understanding attack vectors, lateral movement, and data exfiltration.

Analysis Techniques

Timeline Analysis

Reconstruct the sequence of events by correlating timestamps across multiple evidence sources. Tools like Plaso/log2timeline can create super timelines from diverse sources.

Memory Analysis

Use Volatility or Rekall to analyze memory dumps for: running/hidden processes, network connections, loaded DLLs, injected code, encryption keys, and malware artifacts that may not exist on disk.

Disk Analysis

Use Autopsy, FTK, or EnCase for disk forensics: file system analysis, deleted file recovery, timeline creation, keyword searching, hash analysis against known malware databases.

Malware Analysis

Static analysis (strings, imports, hashes) and dynamic analysis (sandbox execution) to understand malware capabilities, C2 infrastructure, and indicators of compromise.

Log Analysis

Correlate logs from SIEM, endpoints, network devices, and applications to trace attacker activity. Focus on authentication events, process execution, network connections, and file access.

Chain of Custody Requirements

Evidence may be needed for legal proceedings, insurance claims, or regulatory investigations. Proper chain of custody ensures evidence is admissible and defensible.

Evidence Documentation

For each piece of evidence, document:

  • Description of the evidence
  • Date and time of collection
  • Location where evidence was collected
  • Name and role of person who collected it

  • Document all analysis actions taken

  • Physical evidence: locked cabinet or safe with limited access
  • Digital evidence: encrypted storage with access logging
  • Maintain backup copies in separate secure location
  • Retain evidence per legal hold requirements (consult Legal)

Recovery Procedures

Detailed restoration and return to operations

Recovery Planning Principles

Recovery is not simply "restore from backup." It requires careful planning to ensure systems are restored securely, in the right order, and with appropriate validation before returning to production.

Recovery Decision Framework

For each affected system, determine the appropriate recovery approach:

  • Clean and Restore: Remove malware, patch vulnerabilities, restore data. Appropriate for minor infections where root cause is understood and fully addressed.
  • Rebuild from Backup: Restore system from known-good backup image. Requires validation that backup predates compromise and isn't infected.
  • Rebuild from Scratch: Deploy fresh OS from golden image, reconfigure, restore only data. Most secure but most time-consuming. Required when compromise is deep or backup integrity is uncertain.
  • Replace Hardware: In rare cases of firmware compromise or physical tampering, hardware replacement may be necessary.

Recovery Prioritization

Recover systems in order of business criticality, considering dependencies:

  1. Tier 1 - Critical Infrastructure: Active Directory, DNS, DHCP, core network services. Nothing else works without these.
  2. Tier 2 - Security Infrastructure: SIEM, EDR, logging, monitoring. Need visibility before restoring production.
  3. Tier 3 - Critical Business Systems: Customer-facing applications, revenue-generating systems, core databases.
  4. Tier 4 - Important Business Systems: Email, collaboration tools, internal applications.
  5. Tier 5 - Standard Systems: Individual workstations, non-critical applications, development environments.

Pre-Recovery Checklist

  • Confirm eradication is complete‚Äîno active attacker presence
  • Verify the attack vector is closed (patched, credentials reset, etc.)
  • Validate backup integrity and confirm backup predates compromise
  • Prepare recovery environment (isolated network segment for testing)
  • Document recovery plan with clear success criteria for each system
  • Identify staff and resources required for recovery operations
  • Establish communication plan for stakeholders during recovery
  • Prepare rollback plan if recovery introduces new issues

Active Directory Recovery Procedures

AD compromise is particularly serious as it affects the entire environment. Recovery requires special procedures.

If AD is Compromised

  1. Assess Compromise Depth:
    • Were domain admin credentials compromised?
    • Was the KRBTGT account compromised (Golden Ticket)?
    • Were domain controllers directly accessed?
    • Were Group Policies modified?
    • Were new privileged accounts created?
  2. KRBTGT Password Reset:
    • Reset KRBTGT password TWICE (invalidates all Kerberos tickets)
    • Wait at least 10 hours between resets (allow replication)
    • This will cause short-term authentication disruptions
    • Coordinate with operations teams before executing
  3. Privileged Account Reset:
    • Reset ALL domain admin and privileged account passwords
    • Reset service account passwords
    • Review and remove any unauthorized admin accounts
    • Review admin group memberships for unauthorized additions
  4. Trust Relationship Review:
    • Review all forest and domain trusts
    • Verify no unauthorized trusts were created
    • Reset trust passwords if any were potentially compromised
  5. Group Policy Review:
    • Review all GPOs for unauthorized changes
    • Check for malicious scripts in GPO startup/logon
    • Restore GPOs from known-good backup if needed
  6. Full AD Rebuild (Extreme Cases):
    • If compromise is complete/unknown, may need full rebuild
    • Create new forest, migrate users and systems
    • This is extremely disruptive‚Äîlast resort only

Database Recovery Procedures

Database recovery requires special attention to data integrity, transaction consistency, and point-in-time recovery capabilities.

Recovery Steps

  1. Assess Database State:
    • Determine if database files are encrypted/corrupted
    • Check transaction log integrity
    • Identify most recent clean backup
    • Determine acceptable recovery point (RPO)
  2. Identify Recovery Point:
    • When did the compromise occur?
    • What is the most recent backup before compromise?
    • Can we use transaction logs for point-in-time recovery?
    • What data loss is acceptable to the business?
  3. Restore in Isolated Environment:
    • Restore database to isolated recovery environment first
    • Validate data integrity and consistency
    • Test application connectivity and functionality
    • Check for any data corruption or malicious modifications
  4. Apply Transaction Logs (if applicable):
    • Apply transaction logs up to point before compromise
    • Verify transactions don't include malicious activity
    • Validate referential integrity after log application
  5. Credential Reset:
    • Reset all database account passwords
    • Reset application service account credentials
    • Review and revoke unnecessary database permissions
    • Update connection strings in all dependent applications
  6. Return to Production:
    • Execute cutover to restored database
    • Monitor closely for issues
    • Verify application functionality
    • Enable enhanced audit logging

Cloud Infrastructure Recovery

  1. IAM Recovery:
    • Delete all compromised access keys
    • Delete unauthorized IAM users and roles
    • Remove unauthorized policy attachments
    • Reset root account credentials and enable MFA
    • Review and reset all user passwords
    • Regenerate all service account keys
  2. Compute Recovery:
    • Terminate unauthorized EC2 instances
    • Restore affected instances from clean AMIs or snapshots
    • Rebuild instances from golden images if snapshots are suspect
    • Review and remediate security group rules
    • Verify no unauthorized scheduled tasks or Lambda functions
  3. Network Recovery:
    • Review and revert VPC configuration changes
    • Remove unauthorized VPC peering connections
    • Review and clean up route tables
    • Verify no unauthorized internet gateways or NAT gateways
  4. Data Recovery:
    • Restore S3 buckets from versioning if data was deleted/modified
    • Restore RDS databases from automated backups or snapshots
    • Verify data integrity after restoration
    • Review and revoke unauthorized public access

Azure Recovery Procedures

  1. Entra ID Recovery:
    • Revoke sessions for compromised users
    • Reset passwords for all affected accounts
    • Remove unauthorized enterprise applications
    • Review and remove unauthorized app registrations
    • Review conditional access policies for modifications

    • Delete unauthorized VMs and resources

Workstation Recovery

For end-user workstations, rebuild is almost always preferred over cleaning.

Standard Workstation Recovery Process

  2. Reimage workstation from approved golden image

Post-Incident Review Process

The post-incident review (PIR), also called lessons learned or post-mortem, is essential for continuous improvement. It should be conducted within 1-2 weeks while memories are fresh, but after the immediate crisis has passed.

PIR Principles

  • Blameless Culture: Focus on process and system failures, not individual blame. People make mistakes when processes allow mistakes.
  • Honesty: Create psychological safety so participants share what really happened, including mistakes.
  • Action-Oriented: Every identified improvement should result in a tracked action item.
  • Inclusive: Include all participants in the response, not just leadership.
  • Documented: Produce a written report that can inform future incidents.

PIR Scheduling

  • Schedule within 1-2 weeks of incident closure
  • Allow 2-3 hours for significant incidents
  • Include all key participants from the response
  • Designate a facilitator (preferably not the IC)
  • Have someone take detailed notes

PIR Agenda Template

1. Incident Summary (15 minutes)

  • Brief overview of what happened
  • Timeline of key events
  • Impact summary (systems, data, business, customers)
  • Resolution summary

2. Timeline Review (30-45 minutes)

  • Walk through detailed timeline from detection to resolution
  • At each step, discuss: What happened? What worked? What didn't?
  • Identify decision points and evaluate decisions made
  • Note any delays or bottlenecks

3. What Went Well (15 minutes)

  • What detection mechanisms worked?
  • What processes were effective?
  • What tools proved valuable?
  • What communication worked well?
  • Who/what deserves recognition?

4. What Could Be Improved (30 minutes)

  • What was the root cause of the incident?
  • What could have prevented it?
  • What slowed down detection?
  • What slowed down response?
  • Where did communication break down?
  • What tools or capabilities were missing?
  • What playbooks or documentation were inadequate?

5. Action Items (30 minutes)

  • For each improvement area, define specific action item
  • Assign owner to each action item
  • Set target completion date
  • Prioritize based on impact and effort
  • Identify any budget or resource requirements

6. Report Writing and Distribution

  • Facilitator produces written report within 1 week
  • Report reviewed by IC and key stakeholders
  • Final report distributed to appropriate audiences
  • Action items entered into tracking system

PIR Questions to Ask

Detection & Analysis

  • How was the incident first detected?
  • How long was the attacker present before detection (dwell time)?
  • Should we have detected it sooner? What was missed?
  • Were alerts generated but not acted upon?
  • How quickly did we validate this was a real incident?
  • Did we correctly assess the severity initially?

Containment

  • How quickly did we contain the threat?
  • Were containment actions effective?
  • Did the attacker evade containment at any point?
  • Did we have the right tools and access for containment?
  • Were there delays in getting necessary approvals?

Communication

  • Was the right team assembled quickly?
  • Were escalations timely and appropriate?
  • Did stakeholders receive timely updates?
  • Were there any communication gaps or confusion?
  • Did external communications (customers, regulators) go smoothly?

Technical Response

  • Did we have the necessary forensic capabilities?
  • Were playbooks followed? Were they helpful?
  • What tools or capabilities were missing?
  • Was evidence properly preserved?
  • How effective was our recovery process?

Root Cause

  • What was the initial attack vector?
  • What vulnerability or weakness was exploited?
  • Why did that vulnerability exist?
  • What security controls failed or were absent?
  • Were there warning signs we should have noticed?

Tabletop Exercises

Scenarios and drills for IR readiness

Tabletop Exercise Overview

Tabletop exercises (TTX) are discussion-based sessions where team members walk through their roles and responses to a hypothetical incident scenario. They're lower cost than full simulations but highly effective for testing plans, identifying gaps, and building team cohesion.

Benefits of Tabletop Exercises

  • Test incident response plans in a low-risk environment
  • Identify gaps in procedures, tools, and capabilities
  • Build relationships and communication among responders
  • Train staff on their roles and responsibilities
  • Validate escalation and communication procedures
  • Meet regulatory and compliance requirements for testing
  • Improve confidence for actual incident response

Exercise Types

  • Tabletop Discussion: Facilitated discussion walking through a scenario. Participants discuss what they would do at each stage.
  • Functional Exercise: Participants actually perform actions (make calls, write emails) but in a controlled environment.
  • Full-Scale Simulation: Live exercise with actual system isolation, recovery operations, etc. Most realistic but most resource-intensive.
  • Red Team Exercise: Actual attack simulation by internal or external red team, testing real detection and response.

Exercise Frequency

  • Tabletop exercises: Quarterly (minimum annually)
  • Functional exercises: Annually
  • Full simulations: Every 2-3 years or after significant changes
  • Red team exercises: Annually if resources permit

Planning a Tabletop Exercise

Pre-Exercise Planning (2-4 weeks before)

  1. Define Objectives: What do you want to test? New playbook, specific scenario, communication flow, decision-making?
  2. Select Scenario: Choose a realistic scenario relevant to your organization's risk profile.
  3. Identify Participants: Include all relevant roles‚ÄîIR team, IT, Legal, Comms, executives. Consider including third parties (IR firm, legal counsel).
  4. Develop Injects: Create scenario updates that will be introduced during the exercise to drive discussion.
  5. Prepare Materials: Scenario document, participant briefing, evaluation criteria.
  6. Schedule and Logistics: Book room/conference line, allocate 2-4 hours, ensure key participants are available.

Exercise Execution

  1. Introduction (15 min): Welcome, objectives, ground rules, roles
  2. Scenario Presentation (10 min): Present initial scenario and context
  3. Module 1 - Detection (30 min): How would you detect this? Walk through initial response.
  4. Inject 1: Introduce complication (scope expands, new IOCs, etc.)
  5. Module 2 - Containment (30 min): Discuss containment strategy and actions.
  6. Inject 2: Introduce additional challenge (media inquiry, regulatory deadline, etc.)
  7. Module 3 - Communications (20 min): Discuss internal and external communications.
  8. Module 4 - Recovery (20 min): Discuss recovery planning and execution.
  9. Hot Wash (30 min): Immediate feedback‚Äîwhat worked, what didn't, key observations.

Post-Exercise Activities

  • Document observations and lessons learned
  • Create action items for identified gaps
  • Update playbooks and procedures based on findings
  • Schedule follow-up exercise to test improvements
  • Report results to leadership and compliance

Key Performance Indicators Explained

Time-Based Metrics

  • Mean Time to Acknowledge (MTTA): Time from alert to human acknowledgment. Measures SOC responsiveness.
  • Mean Time to Contain (MTTC): Time from detection to successful containment of the threat. Critical for limiting blast radius.
  • Mean Time to Eradicate (MTTE): Time from containment to complete removal of threat from environment.
  • Mean Time to Recover (MTTR): Time from containment to full restoration of normal operations.
  • Dwell Time: Total time attacker was present before detection. Industry average is 200+ days‚Äîgoal is under 24 hours.

Volume Metrics

  • Incident Volume: Total number of incidents over time. Trending up may indicate better detection or increased attacks.
  • Incidents by Severity: Distribution across severity levels. Too many SEV-1s indicates control issues.
  • Incidents by Type: Categories of incidents (malware, phishing, insider, etc.) to inform prevention focus.
  • Incidents by Vector: How incidents originated (email, web, VPN, etc.) to prioritize defenses.
  • Repeat Incidents: Incidents caused by the same root cause as previous incidents. Should be zero‚Äîindicates lessons not learned.

Quality Metrics

  • SLA Compliance: Percentage of incidents handled within defined response timeframes by severity.
  • False Positive Rate: Percentage of alerts that weren't real incidents. Target <10%.
  • Escalation Accuracy: Percentage of escalations that were appropriate. Measures triage quality.
  • Containment Effectiveness: Percentage of incidents where containment prevented further spread.
  • Recovery Success Rate: Percentage of recoveries completed without issues.

  • Playbook Usage: Percentage of incidents where playbooks were followed.
  • Documentation Completeness: Quality score for incident documentation.
  • PIR Completion Rate: Percentage of significant incidents with completed post-incident reviews.
  • Action Item Completion: Percentage of PIR action items completed on time.

  • Downtime: Total hours of system/service unavailability due to incidents.

Recovery Procedures

Restoring systems to normal operations

Recovery Planning

Recovery should not begin until containment is complete and eradication is verified. Premature recovery risks reinfection or continued attacker presence.

Pre-Recovery Checklist

  • Confirm all attacker access has been removed
  • Verify no persistence mechanisms remain
  • Ensure initial access vector has been closed
  • Confirm compromised credentials have been reset
  • Validate backup integrity (backups predate compromise)
  • Obtain Technical Lead and IC approval to proceed

Recovery Prioritization

Recover systems in order of business criticality:

  1. Tier 1 (Critical): Core infrastructure (AD, DNS, DHCP), security tools (SIEM, EDR), customer-facing production systems
  2. Tier 2 (High): Business-critical applications, email, primary databases, communication systems
  3. Tier 3 (Medium): Internal applications, secondary systems, development environments
  4. Tier 4 (Low): Non-essential systems, can remain offline during recovery

Recovery Options

Option A: Restore from Backup

Preferred when clean backups are available that predate the compromise.

  • Verify backup integrity and that it predates initial compromise
  • Restore to clean hardware or freshly provisioned VM
  • Apply all security patches before reconnecting to network
  • Reset local credentials before enabling access
  • Validate functionality in isolated environment before production
Option B: Rebuild from Golden Image

Preferred when backups may be compromised or for standardized systems.

  • Deploy from known-good golden image or automated build
  • Apply current security patches and configurations
  • Install required applications from trusted sources
  • Restore user data from backup (scan for malware first)
  • Rejoin to domain with fresh computer account
Option C: Clean and Repair

Only for minor incidents where full rebuild is unnecessary. Not recommended for sophisticated attacks.

  • Remove identified malware and attacker artifacts
  • Repair any system damage or configuration changes
  • Apply patches for exploited vulnerabilities
  • Run full security scan to verify clean state
  • Monitor closely for signs of remaining compromise

Recovery Execution Steps

  1. Prepare recovery environment (clean network segment if needed)
  2. Restore/rebuild system using chosen method
  3. Apply all current security patches
  4. Harden configuration per security standards
  5. Install and verify EDR agent is active
  6. Enable enhanced logging
  7. Test functionality in isolation
  8. Obtain approval to reconnect to network
  9. Reconnect with monitoring in place
  10. Validate normal operations
  11. Document recovery completion

Post-Recovery Monitoring

Maintain enhanced monitoring for 30-90 days after recovery:

  • Increase logging verbosity on recovered systems
  • Create specific alerts for TTPs observed in this incident
  • Monitor for reinfection or attacker return
  • Watch for lateral movement to recovered systems
  • Schedule follow-up scans and health checks
  • Gradually reduce enhanced monitoring as confidence grows

Key Performance Indicators

Detection Metrics

  • Mean Time to Detect (MTTD): Average time from incident occurrence to detection. Lower is better‚Äîundetected incidents cause more damage.
  • Detection Source Distribution: Percentage of incidents detected by automated tools vs. user reports vs. external notification. More automated detection indicates mature capabilities.
  • False Positive Rate: Percentage of alerts that turn out not to be real incidents. High rates cause alert fatigue‚Äîtarget under 10%.

Response Metrics

  • Mean Time to Acknowledge (MTTA): Time from alert to first responder action. Should be under 15 minutes for critical alerts.
  • Mean Time to Contain (MTTC): Time from detection to stopping active threat. Critical for limiting blast radius.
  • Mean Time to Eradicate: Time from containment to complete threat removal.
  • Mean Time to Recover (MTTR): Time from containment to full restoration of normal operations.

Quality Metrics

  • SLA Compliance: Percentage of incidents handled within defined response timeframes by severity level.
  • Reinfection Rate: Percentage of incidents where the same or similar attack recurs within 90 days. Indicates incomplete eradication.
  • Post-Incident Review Completion: Percentage of SEV-1/SEV-2 incidents with completed post-mortems within 2 weeks.
  • Action Item Completion: Percentage of post-incident action items completed on time.

Volume Metrics

  • Incident Volume by Severity: Total incidents per period broken down by SEV level. Track trends over time.
  • Incident Volume by Type: Distribution across phishing, malware, unauthorized access, etc. Identifies threat focus areas.
  • Incidents per Employee: Normalized metric for comparing across organization sizes or tracking as company grows.

Cost Metrics

  • Average Cost per Incident: Total IR costs divided by incident count. Include labor, tools, external resources.
  • Downtime Cost: Business impact of system unavailability during incidents.
  • External Resource Spend: Costs for external IR firms, legal counsel, notification services.

Reporting Cadence

  • Weekly: Incident count, active incidents, SLA status (operational level)
  • Monthly: MTTD/MTTC/MTTR trends, incident types, lessons learned summary (management level)
  • Quarterly: Comprehensive metrics review, trend analysis, program improvements (executive/board level)

External Resources

When and how to engage outside help

External IR Firms

Consider engaging external incident response firms for:

  • Surge capacity when internal team is overwhelmed
  • Specialized expertise (nation-state attacks, complex forensics)
  • Independent investigation (insider threat, legal proceedings)
  • 24/7 coverage when internal team lacks capacity
  • Regulatory investigations requiring third-party validation

Retainer Recommendations

Establish retainer relationships BEFORE you need them:

  • Negotiate rates and SLAs when not under pressure
  • Pre-approve scoping and engagement terms
  • Ensure NDA and legal terms are already in place
  • Provide firm with environment documentation in advance
  • Test the relationship through tabletop exercises

Law Enforcement

Consider engaging law enforcement for:

  • Significant financial theft or fraud
  • Nation-state or APT attacks
  • Attacks affecting critical infrastructure
  • When you may want criminal prosecution
  • Ransomware attacks (FBI tracks ransomware operators)

Key Contacts

  • FBI: Local field office (find at fbi.gov/contact-us/field-offices) or IC3 (ic3.gov)
  • Secret Service: For financial crimes and critical infrastructure
  • CISA: For critical infrastructure incidents (cisa.gov)
  • Local law enforcement: May be required for some types of incidents

  • Breach coach: Many policies include access to a breach coach (usually an attorney) who coordinates response

Industry Resources

  • CISA: Free resources, alerts, and in some cases direct support for significant incidents
  • Vendor Support: Your security vendors (EDR, SIEM, cloud provider) often have IR support options

Best Practices

Do's and Don'ts for incident response

‚úì Do's
  • Prepare before incidents happen: Have tested playbooks, trained teams, established relationships with external resources, and documented contact information ready.
  • Preserve evidence before remediation: Capture memory, disk images, and logs before taking any cleanup actions. Evidence may be crucial for legal, insurance, or future prevention.
  • Establish clear command structure: One Incident Commander with decision authority prevents confusion and delays. Know who can make what decisions.
  • Document everything in real-time: Assign a dedicated Scribe to maintain timeline, decisions, and rationale. Memory fades; documentation persists.
  • Communicate regularly: Provide status updates even when there's no new information. Set expectations for update frequency. Silence breeds anxiety.
  • Involve legal early: Engage counsel immediately for guidance on privilege, notifications, and evidence handling. Earlier is always better.
  • Follow your playbooks: They exist for a reason. If you find gaps, note them for post-incident improvement.
  • Contain before eradicating: Stop the bleeding first. Understand the scope before trying to remove the threat.
  • Verify eradication completeness: Check for persistence mechanisms, additional compromised accounts, and lateral movement before declaring victory.
  • Conduct blameless post-mortems: Focus on process and system improvements, not individual fault. Create psychological safety for honest discussion.
  • Track and complete action items: Lessons learned are worthless if improvements aren't implemented. Hold people accountable for completion.
  • Run regular exercises: Practice makes permanent. Tabletop exercises reveal gaps and build team confidence before real incidents.
‚úó Don'ts
  • Don't panic or make hasty decisions: Take a breath, follow the playbook, think before acting. Rushed decisions often make things worse.
  • Don't power off systems immediately: This destroys volatile evidence in memory including running processes, network connections, and potentially encryption keys.
  • Don't alert attackers before containment is ready: Tipping off attackers may cause them to accelerate damage or destroy evidence.
  • Don't communicate externally without legal review: Statements can create liability. All external communications should be reviewed by counsel.
  • Don't work in silos: IR requires coordination across security, IT, legal, communications, and business. Share information appropriately.
  • Don't assume the incident is over when immediate threat is contained: Attackers often have multiple persistence mechanisms. Verify thoroughly.
  • Don't skip the post-mortem because everyone is exhausted: Schedule it now while memories are fresh, even if you do it in a week.
  • Don't blame individuals in post-mortems: Blame shuts down honest discussion and prevents real learning. Focus on systemic improvements.
  • Don't delete logs or evidence during remediation: Even accidental deletion can hamper investigation or create legal issues.
  • Don't negotiate with ransomware attackers without expert guidance: Engage professional negotiators through legal counsel if considering payment.
  • Don't let perfect be the enemy of good: Make progress, even if imperfect. You can refine as you go.
  • Don't forget to take care of your team: IR is stressful. Ensure people take breaks, rotate on-call, and recover after major incidents.
