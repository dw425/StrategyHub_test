Change Frameworks

Structured methodologies for planning and executing organizational change. Each framework offers different trade-offs in speed, value delivery, and long-term sustainability.

üî∂

ADKAR Model

Prosci ‚Ä¢ Individual change framework ‚Ä¢ 1998

ADKAR is a goal-oriented change management model that focuses on individual change as the foundation for organizational change. Created by Prosci founder Jeff Hiatt, it provides a sequential framework where each element must be substantially achieved before moving to the next. ADKAR's power lies in its diagnostic capability‚Äîit helps identify exactly where individuals are stuck in the change process, enabling targeted interventions rather than blanket training.

The model treats organizational change as the sum of individual changes. If you can move each person through the five stages, the organization transforms. This bottom-up approach makes it particularly effective for technology adoptions where individual behavior change is the primary success factor.

üîÑ ADKAR Change Model

Awareness
Understanding why change is needed. Business reasons, risks of not changing.
D
Desire
Personal motivation to support and participate in the change.
K
Knowledge
Information on how to change. Training, education, new skills.
A
Ability
Demonstrated capability to implement. Practice and coaching.
R
Reinforcement
Sustaining the change. Recognition, rewards, accountability.
‚úÖ Advantages
  • Highly measurable ‚Äî Each stage can be assessed with surveys, enabling data-driven decisions
  • Pinpoint diagnosis ‚Äî Identifies exactly where individuals are stuck (awareness vs. desire vs. ability)
  • Scalable assessment ‚Äî Works for 10 people or 10,000 with consistent methodology
  • Clear intervention mapping ‚Äî Each barrier point has specific remediation tactics
  • Fast time-to-value ‚Äî Can show measurable progress within weeks, not months
  • Extensive ecosystem ‚Äî Certification programs, tools, templates, benchmarking data available
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Oversimplified sequence ‚Äî Real change isn't always linear; people cycle back through stages
  • Ignores systemic issues ‚Äî Focuses on individuals while organizational structure may be the real blocker
  • Assessment fatigue ‚Äî Requires ongoing surveys that can annoy employees if overused
  • Expensive certification ‚Äî Prosci training costs $3,500-5,000 per person
  • Limited cultural depth ‚Äî Doesn't address deep organizational culture or politics
  • Proprietary lock-in ‚Äî Best tools and benchmarks require ongoing Prosci subscription

Kotter's 8 Steps

John Kotter ‚Ä¢ Harvard Business School ‚Ä¢ 1996

Kotter's model emerged from studying why 70% of change initiatives fail. His research at Harvard identified common patterns in failed transformations: lack of urgency, weak coalitions, unclear vision, under-communication, and declaring victory too soon. The 8-step model addresses each failure mode with a prescriptive action.

Unlike ADKAR's individual focus, Kotter emphasizes organizational momentum. Change succeeds when leaders create a sense of urgency, build powerful coalitions, and generate quick wins that prove the new direction works. It's fundamentally a leadership-driven, top-down approach that relies on executive sponsorship and visible champions.

1
Create Urgency
Help others see the need for change through a bold, aspirational opportunity statement. Without urgency, people won't make the effort.
2
Build Coalition
Assemble a group with enough power, credibility, and influence to lead the change. Include key stakeholders and informal leaders.
3
Form Vision
Create a clear picture of the future that's easy to communicate. If you can't describe it in 5 minutes, it's not clear enough.
4
Enlist Army
Communicate the vision relentlessly through every channel. Large-scale change requires many people urgently wanting it.
5
Enable Action
Remove barriers‚Äîoutdated processes, resistant managers, misaligned incentives. Make it easy for people to act on the vision.
6
Quick Wins
Create visible, unambiguous successes within 6-18 months. Wins build momentum and silence critics.
7
Sustain
Use credibility from wins to change systems, structures, and policies. Don't declare victory too early.
8
Institute
Anchor changes in organizational culture. Connect new behaviors to success. Hire and promote accordingly.
‚úÖ Advantages
  • Research-backed ‚Äî Derived from studying actual transformation failures and successes
  • Addresses political reality ‚Äî Coalition-building acknowledges that change requires power and influence
  • Creates momentum ‚Äî Quick wins approach builds energy and silences skeptics early
  • Culture-aware ‚Äî Final steps explicitly address embedding change in organizational DNA
  • Executive-friendly ‚Äî Language and approach resonates with senior leaders
  • Prevents premature celebration ‚Äî Warns against declaring victory before change is anchored
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Top-down bias ‚Äî Assumes change flows from leadership; may miss ground-level insights
  • Urgency burnout ‚Äî Constant crisis messaging exhausts people over long transformations
  • Slow to start ‚Äî Building coalitions and forming vision takes months before action
  • Hard to measure ‚Äî "Urgency" and "coalition strength" are subjective and qualitative
  • Assumes executive alignment ‚Äî Falls apart when C-suite is divided on the change
  • Sequential rigidity ‚Äî Real transformations often require parallel or iterative steps

Lewin's Change Model

Kurt Lewin ‚Ä¢ Social psychologist ‚Ä¢ 1940s

Kurt Lewin is considered the father of social psychology and his Unfreeze-Change-Refreeze model remains the conceptual foundation for all modern change frameworks. Lewin viewed organizational behavior as a balance of forces‚Äîdriving forces pushing for change and restraining forces maintaining the status quo.

The key insight is that change requires unfreezing first. You can't pour new water into a frozen ice cube tray‚Äîyou must melt what's there. This means creating dissatisfaction with the current state, reducing attachment to existing ways, and building psychological safety for the transition. Only then can change occur, followed by refreezing the new state into permanence.

üßä
Unfreeze
Prepare the organization for change. Challenge the status quo. Create dissatisfaction with current state. Reduce restraining forces. Build psychological safety for what's ahead. This stage is often underestimated‚Äîskipping it dooms the change effort.
üîÑ
Change
Implement the new way of working. This is the transition period where confusion is normal and support is critical. Provide training, coaching, and resources. Model new behaviors. Allow time for learning and adjustment. Accept that productivity may temporarily dip.
üèîÔ∏è
Refreeze
Stabilize the new state. Make the change permanent through policy updates, process documentation, performance metrics, and cultural reinforcement. Celebrate successes. Hire and promote people who embody the new way. Remove those who actively resist.
‚úÖ Advantages
  • Elegant simplicity ‚Äî Three stages are easy to understand, remember, and communicate
  • Force field analysis ‚Äî Companion tool identifies specific drivers and blockers
  • Emphasizes preparation ‚Äî Unfreezing concept prevents rushing into change unprepared
  • Universal applicability ‚Äî Concepts apply to any change in any context
  • No licensing cost ‚Äî Public domain framework with no certification required
  • Foundation for others ‚Äî Understanding Lewin helps you understand all other frameworks
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Too simple for complexity ‚Äî Modern organizations need more detailed guidance
  • Refreeze is outdated ‚Äî In continuous change environments, there's no stable end state
  • No implementation detail ‚Äî Tells you what to do but not how to do it
  • Ignores individual psychology ‚Äî Treats organizations as monoliths, not collections of people
  • Hard to measure progress ‚Äî When is something "unfrozen enough" to change?
  • Linear assumption ‚Äî Real change is iterative, not sequential

McKinsey 7S Framework

McKinsey & Company ‚Ä¢ Tom Peters & Robert Waterman ‚Ä¢ 1980s

The 7S model emerged from McKinsey's research into what makes companies excellent. It views organizations as systems of seven interdependent elements that must be aligned for effectiveness. The insight is that changing one element without considering the others creates dysfunction‚Äîlike upgrading your strategy without updating your structure or skills.

The model distinguishes between "hard" elements (Strategy, Structure, Systems) that are tangible and easily defined, and "soft" elements (Shared Values, Style, Staff, Skills) that are less tangible but equally important. Shared Values sits at the center, connecting everything. The 7S is primarily a diagnostic tool for understanding organizational alignment, not a prescriptive change process.

Hard
Strategy
Plan for competitive advantage‚Äîwhere you play, how you win
Hard
Structure
Organizational hierarchy, reporting lines, divisions, coordination
Hard
Systems
Daily processes, IT, workflows, decision-making procedures
Soft
Style
Leadership approach, management behavior, cultural norms
Core
Shared Values
The fundamental beliefs, principles, and purpose the organization was built on ‚Äî the central element that connects and influences all others
Soft
Staff
Workforce composition, talent management, recruitment, development
Soft
Skills
Core competencies, distinctive capabilities, what the organization does best
‚úÖ Advantages
  • Holistic perspective ‚Äî Forces consideration of all organizational dimensions, not just strategy
  • Reveals interdependencies ‚Äî Shows how changes in one area ripple across others
  • Balances hard and soft ‚Äî Legitimizes culture and style as strategic factors
  • Excellent for M&A ‚Äî Reveals cultural misalignments before they become disasters
  • Versatile application ‚Äî Works for diagnosis, planning, and post-change assessment
  • Executive credibility ‚Äî McKinsey brand carries weight in boardrooms
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Descriptive not prescriptive ‚Äî Tells you what to analyze, not how to change it
  • No sequence or process ‚Äî Doesn't provide a roadmap for transformation
  • Soft elements are fuzzy ‚Äî "Style" and "Shared Values" hard to define and measure
  • Analysis paralysis risk ‚Äî Can spend months mapping current state without acting
  • Complex interdependencies ‚Äî Understanding 21 pairwise relationships is overwhelming
  • Static snapshot ‚Äî Doesn't account for how elements evolve over time

Bridges' Transition Model

William Bridges ‚Ä¢ Organizational consultant ‚Ä¢ 1991

Bridges makes a crucial distinction: change is situational (new org structure, new system, new boss) but transition is psychological (the internal process of adapting). Organizations focus on change; Bridges focuses on transition. His key insight: people don't resist change‚Äîthey resist the losses that change brings.

The model acknowledges that every beginning requires an ending. Before people can embrace the new, they must let go of the old. The "neutral zone" in between is uncomfortable but essential‚Äîit's where confusion reigns but also where creativity emerges. Rushing through the neutral zone is the most common mistake leaders make.

üåÖ
Ending
Every new beginning starts with an ending. People must let go of the old way‚Äîtheir identity, relationships, competence, territory, meaning. Acknowledge what's being lost. Allow grieving. Don't minimize the ending or pretend it's not happening. The pain is real.
üå´Ô∏è
Neutral Zone
The uncomfortable in-between. The old is gone but the new isn't working yet. Confusion, anxiety, lowered morale are normal. But this is also fertile ground‚Äîcreativity and innovation emerge here. Create temporary structures. Celebrate small wins. Don't rush through it.
üåÑ
New Beginning
People develop new identity, energy, and purpose aligned with the change. Beginnings require the 4 P's: Purpose (why), Picture (what it looks like), Plan (how we get there), and Part (their role). Celebrate the new. Reinforce new behaviors consistently.
‚úÖ Advantages
  • Human-centered ‚Äî Addresses the emotional reality of change that other models ignore
  • Explains resistance ‚Äî Reframes "resistance" as grief over loss, which is manageable
  • Validates the messy middle ‚Äî Legitimizes the neutral zone confusion instead of pretending it away
  • Complements other frameworks ‚Äî Can be layered on top of ADKAR, Kotter, or any other approach
  • Applies to individuals and teams ‚Äî Works for one person or a thousand
  • No certification required ‚Äî Books and resources widely available
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Hard to operationalize ‚Äî How do you know when someone has "ended" or is in the neutral zone?
  • Difficult to measure ‚Äî Progress through stages is subjective and qualitative
  • Requires emotional intelligence ‚Äî Leaders must be comfortable with feelings and grief
  • Can slow things down ‚Äî "Honoring the ending" and "sitting in the neutral zone" delays action
  • No project structure ‚Äî Doesn't provide milestones, deliverables, or timelines
  • Individual variation ‚Äî Different people move through stages at different speeds

Choosing the Right Framework

A practical guide for every situation

üí° The Truth About Framework Selection
No single framework works for everything. The most successful change practitioners combine frameworks‚Äîusing Kotter for organizational momentum, ADKAR for individual adoption, Bridges for emotional support, and McKinsey 7S for alignment checks. The key is matching your primary framework to the nature of your change, then supplementing with others to fill gaps.
1
Match to Change Type
Technology change? ADKAR. Cultural transformation? Kotter. High-emotion restructuring? Bridges. Choose the framework designed for your specific challenge.
2
Consider Your Timeline
Need results in weeks? ADKAR or Lewin. Planning a multi-year transformation? Kotter or 7S. Don't use a slow framework for urgent change.
3
Assess Your Resources
Limited budget? Use Lewin or Bridges. Have consulting support? Consider 7S or certified Prosci practitioners. Match framework to resources.
üöÄ When to Use Which Framework
Match your primary challenge to the right approach:
  • Individual behavior change ‚Äî ADKAR as primary, Bridges for emotional support
  • Organizational transformation ‚Äî Kotter as primary, ADKAR for adoption tracking
  • High-emotion change ‚Äî Bridges as primary, Lewin for conceptual framing
  • M&A integration ‚Äî McKinsey 7S for diagnosis, Bridges for cultural transition
  • Quick process change ‚Äî Lewin as primary, ADKAR for individual tracking
  • Technology rollout ‚Äî ADKAR as primary, Kotter for executive sponsorship
‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes to Avoid
Pitfalls that derail framework implementation:
  • Using Kotter for tech rollouts ‚Äî 8 steps is overkill; use ADKAR instead
  • Ignoring emotions in restructuring ‚Äî Bridges is essential when people lose jobs or roles
  • Skipping the "unfreeze" ‚Äî People resist because they're not prepared for change
  • Over-analyzing with 7S ‚Äî Don't spend 6 months mapping current state without acting
  • Treating frameworks as religion ‚Äî Adapt and combine; no framework is perfect
  • Measuring the wrong things ‚Äî Use ADKAR metrics for adoption, not Kotter phases
