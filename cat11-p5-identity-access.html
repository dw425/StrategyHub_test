What is Identity & Access Management (IAM)?

Identity and Access Management (IAM) is the security discipline that ensures the right individuals have appropriate access to the right resources at the right time for the right reasons. IAM encompasses the policies, processes, and technologies used to manage digital identities and control access to systems, applications, and data.

The IAM Challenge for Data Platforms

Modern data platforms present unique IAM challenges: thousands of users with varying needs, sensitive data requiring granular access controls, service accounts for automated pipelines, external partner access, and regulatory requirements for access governance. Without proper IAM, organizations face data breaches, compliance violations, and operational inefficiencies.

Authentication vs. Authorization

IAM consists of two fundamental processes that work together:

  • Authentication (AuthN): Verifying that users are who they claim to be. "Who are you?" This is typically done through passwords, biometrics, tokens, or certificates. Authentication establishes identity.
  • Authorization (AuthZ): Determining what an authenticated user is allowed to do. "What can you do?" This involves checking permissions, roles, and policies to grant or deny access to specific resources.

The Principle of Least Privilege

The foundation of good IAM is the principle of least privilege: users should have only the minimum access necessary to perform their job functions. This limits the blast radius of compromised accounts and reduces the risk of accidental or intentional data exposure. Implementing least privilege requires continuous review and adjustment as roles and responsibilities change.

Key IAM Metrics to Track

Effective IAM requires continuous monitoring of key metrics that indicate the health and security of your identity infrastructure:

  • MFA Adoption Rate: Percentage of users with multi-factor authentication enabled. Target: 100% for humans, risk-based for service accounts.
  • Orphaned Accounts: Accounts belonging to users who have left the organization. Should be zero‚Äîorphaned accounts are a major breach vector.
  • Dormant Accounts: Accounts that haven't been used in 90+ days. Review for removal or extended inactivity justification.
  • Privileged Access Ratio: Percentage of users with admin/elevated access. Lower is better‚Äîtypically under 5% is healthy.
  • Access Review Completion: Percentage of required access reviews completed on time. Target: 100% within SLA.
  • Failed Authentication Rate: Percentage of login attempts that fail. Spikes may indicate attacks or user friction.

Choosing the Right Authentication Method

The best authentication method depends on your security requirements, user population, and technical environment:

  • Human Users (Internal): SSO with MFA mandatory. Consider passwordless for improved security and UX.
  • Human Users (External/Customers): Risk-based MFA with passwordless options. Balance security with friction.
  • Service Accounts: Certificate-based authentication or managed identities. Never use passwords in code.
  • Machine-to-Machine: mTLS with certificate rotation. Consider short-lived tokens from identity providers.
  • Privileged Access: Hardware security keys (FIDO2) plus additional factors. No SMS or email OTP.

Choosing an Authorization Model

Most organizations use a combination of models based on their specific needs:

  • RBAC: Best for stable organizational structures with well-defined job functions. Start here‚Äîit covers 80% of use cases and is easiest to manage.
  • ABAC: Best when access decisions depend on multiple dynamic factors like time, location, or data classification. Add on top of RBAC for fine-grained control.
  • ReBAC: Best for collaborative platforms, document management, or any application where access flows from ownership and sharing relationships.

Data Platform Authorization

For data platforms like Databricks, Snowflake, or BigQuery, implement layered authorization: RBAC for workspace/catalog access, ABAC for row/column-level security based on data classification, and dynamic data masking for sensitive fields. Always prefer deny-by-default and explicit grants over broad permissions.

Why Lifecycle Management Matters

Poor identity lifecycle management is a leading cause of security incidents. When employees leave but accounts remain active (orphaned accounts), attackers have a ready-made entry point. When employees change roles but keep old permissions (access creep), the principle of least privilege erodes over time.

Automation is Essential

Manual lifecycle management doesn't scale. Integrate your identity provider with HR systems to automatically trigger provisioning on hire, access changes on transfers, and deprovisioning on termination. The goal is zero-delay deprovisioning‚Äîaccounts should be disabled within minutes of termination notification, not days or weeks.

Why PAM is Critical

Privileged accounts‚Äîadministrators, root users, service accounts with elevated permissions‚Äîare the most valuable targets for attackers. A compromised privileged account can lead to complete system takeover, data exfiltration, and persistent access that's hard to detect.

PAM Best Practices

  • Eliminate standing privileges: No one should have permanent admin access. Use just-in-time elevation for specific tasks.
  • Vault all credentials: Privileged passwords should never be known by humans or stored in plaintext.
  • Require MFA + approval: Privileged access should require hardware MFA and manager/peer approval.
  • Record everything: All privileged sessions should be recorded for forensic and compliance purposes.
  • Monitor behavior: Use analytics to detect anomalous privileged activity that may indicate compromise.

Zero Trust for Data Platforms

Traditional perimeter-based security assumed everything inside the network was trusted. Zero Trust recognizes that threats can come from anywhere‚Äîincluding inside the network‚Äîand that the perimeter has dissolved with cloud and remote work.

For data platforms, Zero Trust means: verify every user and service accessing data, regardless of location. Don't trust the network‚Äîencrypt all connections. Assume any component could be compromised and limit blast radius through segmentation. Continuously monitor for anomalous access patterns.

Best Practices

IAM do's and don'ts

‚úì Do's
  • Require MFA for all human users without exception‚Äîhardware keys for privileged access
  • Implement SSO to reduce password sprawl and centralize authentication control
  • Automate provisioning/deprovisioning with HR system integration for zero-delay offboarding
  • Use managed identities or workload identity federation for service accounts‚Äîno passwords
  • Conduct quarterly access reviews with manager attestation and auto-revocation for non-response
  • Implement just-in-time privileged access‚Äîeliminate standing admin privileges
  • Apply least privilege by default‚Äîexplicit grants only, deny everything else
  • Monitor and alert on authentication anomalies: impossible travel, unusual times, failed attempts
  • Encrypt all authentication traffic and store credentials in vaults, never in code
  • Document all roles and permissions with clear business justification
‚úó Don'ts
  • Allow shared accounts or credentials‚Äîevery identity should be individual and accountable
  • Use SMS for MFA‚Äîit's vulnerable to SIM swapping and interception attacks
  • Store passwords, API keys, or secrets in source code, config files, or environment variables
  • Grant standing privileged access‚Äîadmin rights should be temporary and just-in-time
  • Rely on self-service access requests without approval workflows and review cycles
  • Keep orphaned accounts after employee termination‚Äîdeactivate within hours, not days
  • Use service accounts interactively or share them across multiple applications
  • Grant broad permissions like "Owner" or "Admin" when specific permissions suffice
  • Skip access reviews because they're time-consuming‚Äîautomate and enforce completion
  • Trust internal network traffic‚Äîapply Zero Trust principles everywhere
