Training & Enablement

Build the knowledge, skills, and confidence people need to succeed with change. Training bridges the gap between "I understand why" and "I know how"‚Äîthe critical ADKAR transition from Knowledge to Ability.

üìä

The 70-20-10 Learning Model

How adults actually learn and retain skills

The 70-20-10 model, developed by the Center for Creative Leadership, shows that adults learn primarily through experience, not formal training. Only 10% of learning comes from courses and classes. The other 90% comes from doing the work and learning from others. This has profound implications for change enablement.

Most organizations over-invest in formal training (the 10%) and under-invest in on-the-job experience (the 70%) and social learning (the 20%). A two-day training class won't create competence‚Äîbut combining training with coaching, practice environments, and peer learning will.

Learning from others. Coaching, mentoring, peer feedback, observing experts, collaboration. Relationships accelerate and contextualize learning.

üéØ
Apply to Change Training
Design training programs that include all three elements: formal instruction (10%), coaching and peer support (20%), and hands-on practice with real work (70%). Training alone won't work.
üîÑ
Sandbox Environments
Create safe spaces to practice. For system changes, build training environments with realistic data. For process changes, run simulations. Mistakes in sandbox = learning. Mistakes in production = pain.
üë•

Connect learners with each other and with experts. Slack channels, lunch-and-learns, office hours with power users. The 20% social learning often gets neglected.
‚úÖ 70-20-10 Advantages
  • Evidence-based ‚Äî Grounded in research on how adults actually develop skills
  • Cost-effective ‚Äî On-the-job learning costs less than formal training programs
  • Contextual ‚Äî Learning happens in the actual work environment with real problems
  • Sustainable ‚Äî Skills built through practice stick better than classroom learning
  • Scalable ‚Äî Peer networks can support more learners than trainers alone
  • Immediate application ‚Äî No gap between "learned it" and "used it"
‚ùå Implementation Challenges
  • Hard to structure ‚Äî Experiential learning is messier than scheduled classes
  • Manager dependency ‚Äî The 70% requires managers who support learning time
  • Quality variance ‚Äî Social learning quality depends on who's teaching whom
  • Measurement difficulty ‚Äî Harder to track informal learning than course completions
  • Foundation still needed ‚Äî Can't skip the 10% formal training entirely
  • Time pressure ‚Äî Organizations often rush past the 70% to "get back to work"

Training Needs Assessment

Identifying the gap between current and required capability

Effective training starts with understanding the capability gap‚Äîthe difference between what people can do today and what the change requires them to do. Without assessment, you'll either over-train (wasting time and money) or under-train (leaving people unprepared).

Needs assessment answers four questions: What do people need to be able to do? What can they do now? What's the gap? And what's the best way to close it? The answers vary by role, location, and individual.

1
Define Target State
What skills, knowledge, and behaviors will people need post-change? Be specific: not "use the new system" but "process customer returns in <3 minutes."
2
Assess Current State
What can people do now? Surveys, skill assessments, manager interviews, performance data. Don't assume‚Äîmeasure.
3
Identify Gaps
Compare target to current. Gaps may be knowledge (don't know), skill (can't do), or motivation (won't do). Each needs different intervention.
4
Prioritize & Plan
Which gaps are biggest? Which roles are most critical? What's the timeline? Design training to address priority gaps first.
üìã
Skills Matrix
Map required skills against roles and current proficiency. Identifies exactly who needs what training. Reveals common gaps across groups and unique gaps for specific roles.
üìä
Role Impact Analysis
Score each role on degree of change: High/Medium/Low. High-impact roles need intensive training. Low-impact roles may need only awareness. Prioritize resources accordingly.
üéØ
Learning Objectives
Write SMART objectives: "After training, participants will be able to [observable action] with [success criteria]." Vague objectives = vague training = vague outcomes.
üí° The Over-Training Trap
Organizations often train everyone on everything "just in case." This wastes time, creates fatigue, and buries critical content in noise. A sales rep doesn't need the same training as a finance analyst. Use role-based curricula: everyone gets what they need, no one gets what they don't. Less training, better targeted, higher retention.

Training Modalities

Different formats for different needs

Training isn't one-size-fits-all. Different content, audiences, and constraints require different modalities. A complex process change might need hands-on workshops. A simple tool update might need just a 5-minute video. Match the modality to the learning objective, audience size, and available resources.

Curriculum Design

Structuring learning pathways by proficiency level

A curriculum is a structured learning pathway that takes learners from current to target proficiency. Good curricula sequence content logically, build skills progressively, and include checkpoints to verify learning. They're not just lists of courses‚Äîthey're designed journeys.

Structure curricula by proficiency level: awareness (knows about it), foundational (can do basics), proficient (works independently), expert (can teach others). Not everyone needs to reach expert level‚Äîmatch the depth to the role requirements.

Level 1: Awareness

Overview of the change and its purpose 15 min

Level 3: Proficient

Proficiency certification 60 min
‚úÖ Structured Curriculum Advantages
  • Progressive skill building ‚Äî Each level builds on the previous, reducing cognitive overload
  • Clear expectations ‚Äî Learners know what they need to complete and why
  • Efficient targeting ‚Äî Not everyone takes everything; match depth to role
  • Measurable progress ‚Äî Checkpoints confirm learning before advancing
  • Easier updates ‚Äî Modular design allows updating parts without rebuilding all
  • Consistent quality ‚Äî Everyone learning the same role gets the same content
‚ùå Potential Pitfalls
  • Development time ‚Äî Building comprehensive curricula takes significant upfront effort
  • Rigidity risk ‚Äî Over-structured paths may not fit all learner needs
  • Maintenance burden ‚Äî Content must be updated as the change evolves
  • Completion focus ‚Äî Risk of optimizing for "finished" over "learned"
  • One-size assumptions ‚Äî Experienced learners may be forced through basics
  • Assessment gaming ‚Äî Checkpoints can be gamed without real learning

Role-Based Training

Tailoring content to what each role actually needs

Different roles interact with change differently. An executive needs strategic context; a frontline user needs button-by-button instructions. A manager needs to understand both and know how to support their team. Role-based training ensures everyone gets exactly what they need‚Äîno more, no less.

üëî
Executive Sponsors
Strategic context & talking points
Executives don't need to use the system‚Äîthey need to champion it. Training focuses on business case, key messages, how to demonstrate visible support, and how to remove barriers for their teams.
  • Strategic rationale and business case
  • Key messages and talking points
  • How to demonstrate visible sponsorship
  • Common questions and objection responses
  • Escalation paths for blockers
üìä
People Managers
Support their teams through change
Managers are the most critical training audience. They must understand the change themselves AND help their teams adopt it. Training covers both the "what" and the "how to help others."
  • Full user training (all relevant levels)
  • How to communicate change to their team
  • Recognizing and addressing resistance
  • Coaching conversations for struggling users
  • How to reinforce and sustain adoption
‚≠ê
Super Users / Champions
Local experts & peer support
Champions receive advanced training so they can support their peers. They're the first line of help before escalating to formal support. Training prepares them to troubleshoot and teach.
  • Expert-level system proficiency
  • Common issues and troubleshooting
  • How to coach and support peers
  • Feedback collection and escalation
  • Early access to updates and new features
üíº
End Users
Day-to-day operators
End users need practical, task-focused training on what changes in their daily work. Skip the strategic context‚Äîfocus on "here's how you do your job now." Make it hands-on and immediately applicable.
  • Role-specific workflows and tasks
  • Step-by-step procedures
  • What's different from before
  • Where to get help
  • Practice in sandbox environment

Post-Training Support

Learning doesn't stop when the class ends

Training creates awareness and initial skill. Support sustains and reinforces learning until the new behavior becomes automatic. Without post-training support, 70% of what's learned in training is forgotten within 24 hours. Support bridges the gap between "trained" and "competent."

The 70% in 70-20-10 happens after formal training ends. On-the-job application, coaching, and just-in-time help are where skills actually develop. Plan support as carefully as you plan training.

Help Desk
Dedicated support team for change-related questions. Staff with trained agents who know the new system/process. Track issues to identify training gaps.
Go-live + 90 days

Experts stationed where users work during initial go-live. Walk around, answer questions, fix issues in real-time. High-touch but high-impact for first weeks.

üë•
Champion Network
Local experts in each team who provide peer support. First line of help before escalating to formal channels. Ongoing role beyond go-live.
Ongoing

Scheduled drop-in sessions where users can ask questions and get live help. Can be virtual or in-person. Creates regular touchpoints for support.
Weekly for 60 days
üìö
Knowledge Base
Searchable repository of how-tos, FAQs, troubleshooting guides. Self-service support available 24/7. Build from actual questions asked.
Ongoing
üîÑ
Refresher Training
Follow-up sessions 30-60 days after initial training. Address gaps identified through support tickets and manager feedback. Cover advanced topics.

üí° The Support Taper
Support intensity should start high and taper gradually. Go-live week might need floor support, daily office hours, and extended help desk hours. By week 4, reduce to office hours twice weekly and normal help desk coverage. By day 90, transition to steady-state support. Pulling support too fast leaves users stranded; keeping it too long creates dependency.

Measuring Training Effectiveness

Beyond completion rates to actual impact

The most common training metric‚Äîcompletion rate‚Äîis nearly useless. Completing a course doesn't mean learning happened. Effective measurement tracks whether training changed behavior and improved performance. Use the Kirkpatrick Model as a framework for multi-level evaluation.

üòä
Level 1: Reaction
Did they like it? Post-training surveys on satisfaction, relevance, engagement. Easy to measure but weakly correlated with actual learning. Useful for improving training design.
üìù
Level 2: Learning
Did they learn it? Knowledge checks, skill assessments, certifications. Pre/post tests show knowledge gained. Still doesn't guarantee on-the-job application.
üéØ
Level 3: Behavior
Are they using it? Observation, manager reports, system usage data, ADKAR assessments. The critical level‚Äîdid training change actual work behavior?
üìä
Level 4: Results
Did it matter? Business metrics: productivity, quality, speed, adoption rates. Hard to isolate training's contribution but ultimate measure of value.
üí∞
Level 5: ROI
Was it worth it? Compare training costs to business results gained. Phillips' addition to Kirkpatrick. Challenging to calculate but powerful for justifying investment.
‚ö°
Leading Indicators
Don't wait for Level 4 to know if training is working. Track support ticket volume, time-to-proficiency, error rates, manager confidence scores. Early signals allow course correction.
‚úÖ Multi-Level Measurement Advantages
  • Complete picture ‚Äî Each level reveals different aspects of training effectiveness
  • Early warning ‚Äî Lower levels flag problems before business impact is measured
  • Targeted improvement ‚Äî Identifies whether problem is content, delivery, or environment
  • ROI justification ‚Äî Higher levels connect training investment to business value
  • Stakeholder credibility ‚Äî Different levels satisfy different stakeholder questions
  • Continuous improvement ‚Äî Data drives iteration on training design
‚ùå Measurement Challenges
  • Increasing difficulty ‚Äî Higher levels are exponentially harder to measure accurately
  • Attribution problems ‚Äî Hard to isolate training's impact from other factors
  • Time lag ‚Äî Behavior and results take weeks/months to manifest
  • Data availability ‚Äî Organizations often lack systems to track behavior and results
  • Survey fatigue ‚Äî Repeated assessments annoy learners and reduce response
  • Cost of measurement ‚Äî Comprehensive evaluation requires significant investment
